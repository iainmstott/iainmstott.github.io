IAIN STOTT

I'm a computational evolutionary ecologist. I study how environmental disturbances change the structures of ecological systems to cause nonstable dynamics, and how that feeds back into system function through developmental and evolutionary processes. This research can also help forecast changes in and inform management of ecological systems. Please see the RESEARCH section for more detail about what this means and how it's achieved!

RESEARCH

POPULATION ECOLOGY

Mostly, my research revolves around the study of populations of plants and animals. My primary methods of inquiry involve:

  • Using mathematical models (especially matrix population models) to explore how environmental disturbances (e.g. extreme weather, natural disasters, fire or disease) change the age or stage structures of populations and effect nonstable 'transient' dynamics (short-term ***fluctuations?*** in population growth and structure).
  • Developing methods, software and apps to analyse both population dynamics and life history of population models, which aside from research applications, can help inform best options for population management.
  • Using comparative statistical analyses (especially variations on Bayesian GLMMs) across many species to link such characteristic disturbance responses to the life histories of organisms, in an effort to further understand eco-evolutionary drivers of life history evolution across the tree of life.
  • Using computer simulations (including individual-based modelling) as a means of validating ideas in silico and providing a springboard for further research.

WHYAGE

Imagine you're on a beautiful Caribbean island. Maybe you are! But maybe you're also at risk of experiencing a disaster. A hurricane is an potential environmental disturbance (other examples include fire, disease, natural disasters or human activities). Coral populations are ecological systems which would be affected by a hurricane: population structure would be changed by a hurricane as it rips older, larger corals from the rock. This change could cause nonstable population dynamics: short-term population growth could be lower, as younger, smaller corals would reproduce less and need to grow in size before population growth stabilises to a pre-hurricane level. Over time, corals might evolve to reproduce at a higher rate when young, in order to recover quickly after hurricanes and use the environmental disturbance to proliferate faster than other intra- or inter-specific individuals. Knowledge of how hurricanes affect coral populations in the short- and long-term could help us to manage them better for their conservation.

The WHYAGE project isn't about corals, but this illustrates the theoretical underpinnings of the project. The project aims to understand the impact of environmental disturbances on population dynamics, mediated through a species' life history and particularly the way in which it ages. In fact, in the coral example the species evolves toward senescence: lower reproduction later in life. This is counterintuitive from an evolutionary point of view!

WHYAGE explores the ecology and evolution of aging by two primary means:
First, understanding how measures of aging impact transient dynamics across the tree of life. To do this, I use the COMPADRE & COMADRE databases which contain mathematical models describing life history and population dynamics (see the DATA section). If certain aging patterns are linked with certain transient responses to disturbance, this could indicate certain tendencies towards certain life histories in certain environments. That's a lot of "certain", but in fact we are completely uncertain what these patterns may look like: whether they are correlated across the phylogeny, related to body type or growth form, or determined by where a population is located. I use comparative Bayesian linear modelling to try and work out where the truth lies.
The second avenue of exploration is simulating populations. This example is just theory, incidentally the theory that my WHYAGE MSCA project is based on.
